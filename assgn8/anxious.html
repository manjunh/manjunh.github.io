Anxious Attachment Style

Some children will commonly exhibit clingy and dependent behavior. When distressed they are difficult to soothe and are not comforted by interaction with the attachment figure. This behavior results from an inconsistent level of response to their needs from the primary caregiver.

John Bowlby's Theory (1969)


Children come into the world biologically pre-programmed to form attachment for survival

Child would initially form only one attachment and that the attachment figure acted as a secure base for exploring the world

Attachment relationship acts as a prototype for all future social relationships so disrupting it can have severe consequences

Strange Situation

In 1969, Ainsworth developed an experimental procedure in order to observe the variety of attachment forms exhibited between mothers and infants. Infants were aged between 12 and 18 months. The sample comprised of 100 middle-class American families.

Mother & baby goes into room and plays around.

A stranger(experimenter) joins the mother and infant.
Stranger Anxiety - Avoids stranger, showing signs of fear

Mom leaves and stranger interacts with the baby.

Mom returns and stranger leaves.
Reunion Behavior - Infant approaches the mother, but resists contact

Mom leaves, leaving the baby completely alone.
Separation Anxiety - Intense distress when the mother leaves

Stranger returns but the baby is avoidant of the stranger.
Stranger Anxiety - Avoids stranger, showing signs of fear

Mom returns and stranger leaves.
Reunion Behavior - Infant approaches the mother, but resists contact, may even push her away

Adulthood

At times, parents exhibit nurturing, caring, and attentive behaviors. Other times they can be cold, rejecting, or emotionally detached. As a result, the children don’t know what to expect and form anxious attachment style. These children display considerable distress when separated from a parent or caregiver, but do not seem reassured or comforted by the return of the parent. This anxious attachment style continues to prolong even after they grow up.

Romantic Relationship

  • Desire a lot of connection within their relationships, sometimes to the point of being “clingy”
  • Highly aware and sensitive of any slight changes in the relationship
  • Worry that their partner does not love them, therfore needs more validation and approval
  • Become very distraught when relationships end

Thoughts

  • 'I want to be extremely emotionally close with others, but others are reluctant to get as close as I would like.'
  • 'I often worry that my partner doesn’t love or value me and will abandon me.'
  • 'My inordinate need for closeness scares people away.'

Population

About 20 percent of people in the world have anxious attachment style.

"None of these attachment styles are labeled 'healthy' or 'unhealthy'. They're simply descriptions of the way you act in romantic relationships. They're not forms of judgment."
